PRATIO: Polycystine Radiolaria As a proxy To explore Issues On global environmental change and plankton ecology, biodiversity and biogeochemistry

(2024-2028)

PRATIO

Polycystine radiolarians (Rhizaria) are one of the main Rhizarian lineages living today in modern oceans and having a fossil record that goes back into the early Cambrian (520 millions years ago). Their delicate siliceous skeletons are used as tracers of past oceanographic processes that reveal the dynamics of past climate changeÂ ; they are also used to illustrate plankton evolution and biodiversity dynamics in marine pelagic ecosystems. Finally, their significant role in contemporary global biogeochemical cycles (e.g., Carbon, Silicon, etc.) has been increasingly acknowledged in the last two decades.

Integration

Currently we are unable to decipher their full potential, since their taxonomy is essentially based on their aesthetically pleasing and minute siliceous skeletons. As their in-depth understanding requires challenging multidisciplinary studies, this project will address a number of scientific challenges through the setup of a specialised and complementary international network of molecular biologists, paleontologists, ecologists and marine biogeochemists.

Objectives

1) Consolidate and further develop the interactions of the various communities gathered together thanks to the InterRad meetings of the International association of radiolarian specialists.
2) Mature the conditions and the appropriate network to generate a convincing proposal for a Marie Curie Doctoral Network.
3) Prepare the next generation of radiolarian scientists through the organisation of a multidisciplinary workshop on an oceanographic vessel or a Marine scientific station.
